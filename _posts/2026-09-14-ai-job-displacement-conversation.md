## Problem

A managing partner at a 60-attorney firm called last quarter. Goldman Sachs economists had estimated AI was wiping out 16,000 net U.S. jobs per month, with Gen Z white-collar workers hit hardest. Two of his first-year associates asked, in the same week, whether their jobs would exist in three years. Lay people off, or lose them because he did not move fast enough?

That call captures the problem. The "AI will take your job" headline is loud and largely wrong for professional services. The opposite headline, "AI will only augment, never replace," is just as misleading. AI is not coming for your partners, but it is reshaping who you hire and what entry-level work looks like. McKinsey's 2025 survey found 51% of organizations said generative AI was lessening their need for entry-level hires. That is not a forecast. It is in your hiring funnel now.

For 20-to-200-person legal, accounting, or consulting firms, the displacement question is no longer theoretical. The decisions you make this quarter will decide whether your firm grows into a leaner, more profitable version, or loses the next generation of talent to a competitor who got the transition right.

## Why It Matters

The scale of the shift is real and recent. Goldman Sachs' August 2025 analysis found unemployment among 20-to-30-year-old workers in tech-exposed occupations had risen almost three percentage points since the start of 2025. By April 2026, the same team estimated net AI-driven job losses at roughly 16,000 per month. CEO David Solomon has publicly called the broader "job apocalypse" framing overblown, but the entry-level white-collar signal is not contested. McKinsey's 2025 survey backs it from the demand side: roughly half of organizations say gen AI has reduced their entry-level hiring plans.

For professional services, the work product is people-hours billed against client matters. PwC's 2026 Global AI Jobs Barometer found entry-level roles highly exposed to AI have flatlined, even as a 56% wage premium has emerged for AI-enabled skills. The Thomson Reuters 2025 *Future of Professionals* report found the average lawyer now expects to save 240 hours per year through AI tools, twelve weeks of associate time annually, and the firms pulling it off are quietly repricing services while everyone else debates whether to buy a license.

There is also a legal risk that is easy to miss. A February 2026 Above the Law analysis of Baker McKenzie's "AI-driven" layoffs made the point clearly: blaming AI for a reduction in force does not insulate a firm from age-discrimination scrutiny. EEOC age-discrimination charges still run into the tens of thousands a year, and experts note that only a small fraction of affected workers ever file a formal complaint. Any firm that lets AI dictate who gets cut, without a defensible business rationale and human review, is building a litigation file it does not want.

## The Displacement Conversation Approach

The way to have an honest displacement conversation with your partners and your people is to treat it as three separate questions stacked on top of each other. Skip a layer and the next one will not hold.

**Layer 1: Reframe from "Who do we cut?" to "What work is changing?"** The first move is to retire the replacement framing. BCG's April 2026 analysis found AI will reshape far more U.S. jobs than it eliminates. In a law firm, the work that changes is not "the paralegal" or "the first-year associate." It is the slice of each role that is high-volume, rules-based, and tolerant of review: contract clause extraction, due diligence checklists, first-draft memos, invoice processing, lease abstracting, tax return preparation. Describe the change in terms of tasks, not titles, and the conversation shifts from layoff math to workflow redesign. Your senior people stop quietly updating their resumes.

**Layer 2: Audit the workflow before you audit the headcount.** The 2024 Clio Legal Trends Report found 69% of hourly billable work performed by paralegals could in principle be automated by AI, a useful ceiling, not a target. Map the actual work in your firm, identify which automatable hours you can responsibly hand to a model with a human reviewer, and measure the delta in hours and error rates before you touch staffing. Wolters Kluwer's March 2026 analysis of agentic AI in accounting recommends the same sequencing: workflow first, automation second, agentic orchestration third, then staff-model decisions. The firms getting this right in 2026 started with a workflow audit and let staffing resolve itself on the other side of measured productivity.

**Layer 3: Decide your talent strategy on purpose, not by drift.** Once the workflow data is in, you face a strategic choice with three legitimate options. The first is "fewer people, same output," the path Baker McKenzie appeared to take in early 2026, which carries the highest legal and morale risk. The second is "same people, higher-value work," the path Allen & Overy took with its Harvey rollout, reinvesting saved hours into advisory work that commands a higher rate. The third is "more people, new services," the pattern the World Economic Forum's 2025 *Future of Jobs Report* identified as dominant: 92 million jobs displaced globally by 2030, 170 million created, net gain of 78 million. Most firms will run a mix, but should choose deliberately and document the rationale, for internal morale and for any ADEA or WARN Act scrutiny.

Measurement is the discipline that holds all three layers together. McKinsey's high-performer data is consistent: organizations that redesign workflows and measure before-and-after have displacement conversations that go well. Organizations that skip measurement end up in court.

## Real-World Examples

**Baker McKenzie's 2026 restructuring (legal, cautionary).** In February 2026, Above the Law and the ABA Journal reported Baker McKenzie was conducting a significant reduction in force and attributing part of the rationale to AI-enabled efficiency. Recruiting experts quoted in both outlets noted AI was almost certainly a contributor and convenient framing rather than the sole cause. If AI becomes the public explanation for a layoff, you invite questions about which jobs, which workers, and which criteria the model was used to evaluate. Document the workflow changes, productivity data, and business rationale independently of the staffing decision.

**Allen & Overy's Harvey rollout (legal, expansionary).** Allen & Overy (now A&O Shearman) was an early major-firm deployer of Harvey, the legal-specific generative AI platform. By late 2025, the firm reported double-digit time savings on first drafts and standard research, with gains concentrated among associates trained on the tool's prompt patterns. The firm did not cut headcount. It reinvested recovered hours into higher-value advisory work and grew margin per lawyer. The Thomson Reuters 2025 report keeps circling this point: AI does not pay for itself through headcount cuts. It pays for itself through repricing and scope.

**The WEF 2025 net-jobs data (cross-industry, structural).** The World Economic Forum's 2025 *Future of Jobs Report*, drawing on responses from over 1,000 global employers representing more than 14 million workers, projected 92 million jobs displaced and 170 million created by 2030, a net gain of 78 million. PwC's 2026 AI Jobs Barometer reached a complementary conclusion: jobs are growing even in roles most exposed to automation, but skills inside those roles are shifting fast. Firms that invest in retraining tend to win. Those that act on the layoff-only scenario regret it within 18 months.

## Action Steps

1. **Run a workflow audit before you have a headcount conversation.** Pick three processes that consume the most hours. Map tasks, time per task, and which tolerate AI assistance with review. Let the data speak first.

2. **Write down what you will and will not automate.** A one-page policy naming workflows, human-review requirements, and data classification rules. Sign as managing partner. It is your first defense if any reduction in force is later challenged.

3. **Measure productivity in hours, not vibes.** Baseline hours and error rate, deploy AI with review, re-measure at 30, 60, and 90 days. A pilot that ships a defensible number expands. One that ships a demo ends.

4. **Have the talent conversation now.** Tell associates and paralegals what is changing and what new work is opening. PwC's 56% wage premium for AI-enabled skills is real; your best people are training themselves. If the firm does not name the path, they will, on their way out.

5. **Document every staffing decision as a business decision.** Age, tenure, role, and AI-exposure should be separable in any reduction-in-force memo. Age-discrimination scrutiny is real. Defensible rationale protects the firm and the partners who sign.

6. **Re-price, do not just cut.** The Thomson Reuters 240-hour savings only converts to firm value if reinvested into higher-rate work or higher-volume capacity. The 2026 win is "AI means better-margin work."
