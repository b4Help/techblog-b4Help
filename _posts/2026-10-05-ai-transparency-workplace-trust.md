## Problem

AI has quietly moved from drafting documents to shaping decisions about people. In 2026, algorithms help screen resumes, rank candidates, flag flight risk, score performance, and even suggest who is ready for promotion. The problem is that most of these systems are opaque to the people they judge. An associate passed over for a stretch assignment, a candidate filtered out before a human ever saw the application, a staff accountant flagged as "disengaged" by a model nobody can explain: each of these is a small fracture in trust. When employees and clients cannot see how a decision was made, they assume the worst, and in 2026 that assumption increasingly comes with a lawyer attached.

## Why It Matters

Transparency is now both a trust issue and a legal one. On the trust side, a 2025 Workday survey found that 83% of employees believe AI will make uniquely human skills more critical, not less, and PwC's 2025 Global Workforce Hopes and Fears survey of nearly 50,000 workers found that trust in leadership is one of the strongest predictors of motivation and retention. A black-box decision is a trust-destroying machine: it tells your best people that their careers are being shaped by something they are not allowed to understand.

On the legal side, the ground has shifted fast. In May 2025, a federal court granted preliminary certification of a nationwide collective action in *Mobley v. Workday*, covering job applicants age 40 and older allegedly screened out by AI hiring tools, making it the most significant AI hiring discrimination case in the country. New York City's Local Law 144 has required bias audits for automated employment decision tools since 2023. And the EU AI Act classifies AI used in recruitment, evaluation, and termination as high-risk, requiring human oversight, bias testing, and disclosure; its Article 86 gives individuals the right to an explanation of decisions made about them by such systems. Even with some high-risk deadlines pushed back by the EU's 2026 Digital Omnibus, the direction is unmistakable: opacity is becoming a liability, and explainability is becoming the floor.

## The AI Approach

Transparency is not a press release. It is a set of design and governance choices baked into how AI touches your people. Five practices separate the firms that build trust from the firms that erode it.

**First, demand explainable outputs.** If a tool ranks a candidate or flags an employee, it must surface the contributing factors in plain language, not a mysterious score. "Flagged because tenure in role exceeds 24 months with no promotion discussion" is a sentence a manager can act on and defend. A number nobody can explain is a number that should not drive a decision about a person.

**Second, disclose that AI is in the loop, and how.** The emerging legal standard, from the EU AI Act to a growing list of U.S. state and city rules, is that affected people have a right to know when AI is being used in a decision about them, what it does, and how to contest the outcome. Tell candidates that AI screens applications. Tell employees what signals feed a retention model and let them opt out of the intrusive ones. Disclosure is not a weakness; it is what makes the rest of the program legitimate.

**Third, keep a human accountable for every consequential decision.** AI can rank, surface, and recommend. A named human must review and decide, and must be able to explain the decision in their own words. This is the same "assistive, not autonomous" principle that high-stakes industries have converged on, and it is the single best protection against both bias and the perception of bias.

**Fourth, audit for disparate impact on a schedule.** Run the numbers quarterly: who advances, who gets flagged, who gets filtered, broken down by the protected categories that matter under EEOC guidance and local law. NYC's bias-audit requirement is the template even where it is not legally mandated. A pattern caught in an internal audit is a problem you can fix; a pattern caught in discovery is a headline.

**Fifth, document everything.** What data feeds the model, who reviewed each consequential decision, when the last bias audit ran, what it found. In 2026, the firms that can show their work are the ones that keep both their people's trust and their regulators' patience.

The through-line is that transparency is not the enemy of efficiency. It is the precondition for using AI on people-decisions at all. A model your team trusts gets used; a model they suspect gets quietly ignored or, worse, litigated.

## Real-World Examples

**Mobley v. Workday (cautionary).** The 2025 collective-action certification turned AI hiring opacity from a theoretical risk into an active, nationwide legal exposure. The lesson for any firm using algorithmic screening is direct: if you cannot explain why a tool rejected someone, you cannot defend the decision.

**New York City Local Law 144.** Since 2023, employers using automated employment decision tools in NYC have had to commission independent bias audits and notify candidates. It is the clearest existing blueprint for what "transparent AI hiring" looks like in practice, and many firms outside the city are adopting the same discipline voluntarily.

**The EU AI Act framework.** By classifying employment AI as high-risk and granting a right to explanation, the EU has set a global reference point. Even firms with no European footprint are using its requirements, human oversight, bias testing, disclosure, as a checklist for defensible AI.

## Action Steps

1. **Inventory every place AI touches a people-decision.** Hiring, evaluation, retention, scheduling, promotion. You cannot govern what you have not mapped.
2. **Write a one-page AI transparency statement.** What AI you use, what it decides, what stays human, and how someone contests an outcome. Share it internally and with candidates.
3. **Require explainability in every vendor contract.** If a tool cannot tell you why, do not deploy it on people.
4. **Schedule a quarterly bias audit.** Treat disparate-impact review as routine, not a response to a complaint.
