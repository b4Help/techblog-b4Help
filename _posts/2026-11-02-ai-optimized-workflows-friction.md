## Problem

Ask any partner where their day went and the honest answer is rarely "the work." It went to the friction around the work: searching three systems for the right document, chasing a status update, rescheduling a meeting for the fourth time, copying numbers from a PDF into a spreadsheet, writing the email about the email. Microsoft's 2025 Work Trend Index put hard numbers on this drag, finding that the average employee spends 57% of their time communicating in meetings, email, and chat versus 43% actually creating, that 62% struggle with the time they spend searching for information, and that workers are interrupted roughly every two minutes during core hours. In a professional services firm, where the product is billable judgment, every hour lost to administrative friction is an hour that never reaches a client or a margin.

## Why It Matters

Administrative friction is the most expensive cost no one budgets for. It does not show up as a line item; it shows up as senior people doing junior work, as deadlines that slip for no good reason, and as the quiet exhaustion of an "infinite workday" that never quite ends. The Microsoft research found that 68% of people say they lack enough uninterrupted focus time, and that after-hours work is climbing. For a firm, that has three consequences. It erodes margin, because expensive professionals spend a majority of their time on tasks a system could do. It erodes quality, because fragmented attention is the enemy of good judgment. And it erodes retention, because the friction is precisely what burns people out. The same firms that have already shown AI can return real capacity, PwC reported its people executed more than 8.7 million Copilot actions and freed over 500,000 hours in a single month, are the ones proving that the friction is not inevitable. It is a design choice.

## The AI Approach

AI-optimized workflow is not "add a chatbot." It is the systematic removal of friction from how work moves through the firm, built in four layers.

**First, collapse the search problem.** The single biggest friction in knowledge work is finding things. An AI assistant connected to the firm's documents, email, and practice-management system answers "where is the latest version of the Henderson engagement letter" or "what did we advise this client on indemnities last year" in seconds, instead of the ten-minute hunt that used to break a person's focus. Returning that time, and that focus, is often the highest-ROI AI move a firm can make.

**Second, automate the connective tissue.** Scheduling, status updates, follow-ups, time entry, intake forms, data transfer between systems: this is the connective tissue that consumes hours and produces nothing billable. AI assistants and lightweight automation now handle most of it, drafting the follow-up, proposing the meeting time, pre-filling the form, extracting the figures. The goal is to delete the task, not to make the human a little faster at it.

**Third, move from single tasks to orchestrated workflows.** The 2026 frontier is agentic AI, where multiple steps are coordinated rather than each prompted by hand. As analyses of agentic accounting describe it, the shift is from "task automation," a model drafting one email, to "workflow orchestration," where AI agents hand work off to each other across an engagement: intake to research to draft to a defined human review. The professionals stop being the people who move work between steps and become the people who own the judgment at the checkpoints.

**Fourth, design the human checkpoints deliberately.** Removing friction is not the same as removing oversight. The firms that get this right specify, before deployment, exactly where a human must review, sign, and own the output, especially anything that touches a client or a regulator. The friction worth keeping is the friction that protects quality; the friction worth deleting is everything else. Done well, AI removes the drudgery and concentrates human attention on the few moments that actually require it.

The compounding payoff is a firm where senior people spend their time on senior work. The recovered hours can be reinvested in client relationships, business development, mentoring, or simply a sustainable workday, which is itself a retention and quality strategy. The friction was never the work. It was everything standing between your people and the work.

## Real-World Examples

**Microsoft's "infinite workday" research.** The 2025 Work Trend Index quantified the friction, a majority of time spent communicating rather than creating, constant interruption, too little focus time, and named the cost. It is the clearest case that administrative drag, not capability, is the real bottleneck in modern professional work.

**PwC's Copilot deployment.** PwC's report of millions of AI actions and hundreds of thousands of recovered hours in a single month shows the upside at scale: when friction is systematically removed, the capacity returned is enormous. The lesson for smaller firms is the discipline of measuring hours recovered, not features adopted.

**Agentic workflow orchestration in accounting.** Industry analyses of mid-sized accounting firms moving from task automation to multi-agent workflow orchestration show the next step: not just faster individual tasks, but coordinated workflows with humans owning the judgment checkpoints. The firms that made it work spent the prior years getting single use cases into production first.

## Action Steps

1. **Time-audit one week.** Have a team log where the hours actually go. The administrative friction will be larger than anyone guesses, and that is your roadmap.
2. **Deploy an AI search assistant first.** Connecting AI to your documents and email to kill the "where is it" problem is usually the fastest, highest-ROI win.
3. **Automate one piece of connective tissue.** Pick scheduling, follow-ups, or time entry and delete the task rather than speeding it up.
4. **Define the human checkpoints before you orchestrate.** Decide where a person must review and sign, then let AI handle everything in between.
